# The Three Ways & the Five Ideals (in depth)

*The Phoenix Project* and *The Unicorn Project* (Gene Kim). The novels' durable content is these principle sets and the operational lenses behind them — use them as a diagnostic for *why* delivery is slow or fragile.

## The Theory of Constraints & the Four Types of Work (Phoenix Project)

The plot turns on Eli Goldratt's **Theory of Constraints**: a system's throughput is governed by its single **bottleneck**; any improvement *not* at the constraint is a mirage (it just builds inventory before the bottleneck). So: **find the constraint, exploit it, subordinate everything else to it, elevate it, then repeat.** In IT the constraint is often a single overloaded person/team or a manual deploy step.

The **four types of work** to make visible and manage:
1. **Business projects** — features delivering business value.
2. **Internal IT projects** — infrastructure, automation, migrations.
3. **Changes** — generated by the above; flow through the change pipeline.
4. **Unplanned work / firefighting** — the most destructive: it's *anti-work* that preempts the other three. The goal is to reduce unplanned work by improving flow and feedback so problems don't reach production.

WIP (work-in-progress) is "the silent killer" — too much WIP, too-large batches, and unplanned work destroy flow.

## The Three Ways

**First Way — Flow (left → right).** Maximize flow of work from Development → Operations → the customer.
- Make work **visible** (kanban boards) and limit **WIP**.
- **Reduce batch sizes** (small, frequent changes over big releases) and shorten lead times.
- **Never pass defects downstream**; build quality in.
- Optimize for **global** goals, not local/departmental efficiency.

**Second Way — Amplify Feedback (right → left).** Create fast, constant feedback at every stage.
- Pervasive **telemetry** so you can see problems immediately.
- **Swarm** problems when they occur to fix and learn (the Andon-cord idea).
- Push **quality and responsibility toward the source** — those who create work catch their own defects.
- Shorten and amplify feedback loops so the next upstream step learns fast.

**Third Way — Continual Learning & Experimentation.**
- Foster a **high-trust, blameless** culture that rewards experimentation and treats failures as learning.
- **Improvement of daily work > daily work itself** — reserve capacity to pay down debt and improve the system.
- Inject **resilience** through practice (game days, chaos drills) and convert local discoveries into global improvements (sharing).

## The Five Ideals (Unicorn Project)

A developer-experience lens that complements the Three Ways:
1. **Locality and Simplicity** — architecture and org structure should let a team make a change **locally**, without cross-team coordination; complexity is the enemy of flow (cf. [[software-design]] deep modules / [[domain-driven-design]] bounded contexts). Loose coupling is a delivery property, not just a design nicety.
2. **Focus, Flow, and Joy** — protect developers' flow state; fast feedback, minimal handoffs/interruptions, and the joy of doing meaningful work productively.
3. **Improvement of Daily Work** — explicitly value improving the system (paying down "the [tech] debt," fixing the tooling) over only shipping features; the famous "data hairball" is what happens when you don't.
4. **Psychological Safety** — people can raise problems, admit mistakes, and take risks without fear; the prerequisite for the Third Way's learning culture (Westrum's generative culture).
5. **Customer Focus** — relentlessly separate **core** (capabilities customers pay for / your differentiator) from **context** (necessary but undifferentiated work — candidates to simplify, buy, or automate). Spend your best people on core.

## Using these as a diagnostic

When delivery is slow or fragile, ask: Where's the **constraint**? How much **unplanned work** are we drowning in? How big are our **batches/WIP**? How fast is **feedback** (do we find defects in dev or in prod)? Is daily-work improvement happening, or only features? Is it **safe** to surface problems? Can a team change one thing **locally**? The answers point at which Way/Ideal to invest in — and usually at concrete practices in [[devops]] (CI/CD, IaC via [[ansible]]) and [[site-reliability-engineering]] (SLOs, blameless postmortems, toil reduction).
