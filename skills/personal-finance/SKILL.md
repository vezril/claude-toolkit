# Personal Finance (money for a good life)

Built from two complementary books. **Andrew Hallam's *Balance: How to Invest and Spend for Happiness, Health, and Wealth* (2022)** supplies the philosophy and investing core: wealth is a means, not the end — the goal is a life with health, relationships, purpose, and *enough* money, built on **low-cost passive investing** and **spending deliberately on what raises well-being**. **Erica Alini's *Money Like You Mean It* (Dundurn, 2021)** adds the **practical, explicitly Canadian toolkit** for today's economy: debt and credit, rent-vs-buy and mortgages, income and side hustles, insurance, wills, and couples/family money. Hallam is global-with-Canadian-accounts; Alini is Canada-specific.

> **Not financial advice.** This skill summarizes one book's principles for education and discussion. Claude is not a financial advisor. It can lay out the factors and trade-offs so a person can decide for themselves, but it should not give confident buy/sell recommendations. Contribution limits, tax rules, and product details change over time and differ by country/province — **verify current specifics** (and suggest a fee-only advisor or accountant for personalized, high-stakes decisions). This is a standalone life/finance skill — the rest of the toolkit is engineering-focused.

## Part 1 — Spend and live for well-being

The behavioural/life half of the book (chapters 1–6, 10, 12):

- **Stuff vs. life (time affluence).** People near the end of life rarely wish they'd worked more or owned more; they value experiences, relationships, and time. Optimize for **time affluence**, not just net worth. A higher salary that costs you health, relationships, or a place you love is often a bad trade (ch. 1–2).
- **Spend on what buys happiness.** Research (Dunn & Norton's *Happy Money* etc.) says money raises well-being most when spent on: **experiences** over things, **other people** (giving/treating), **buying time** (outsourcing chores you dislike), **many small treats** over rare big ones, and **anticipation** (paying ahead, savoring). Adaptation dulls the joy of possessions; novelty and scarcity restore it (so have the luxury *less* often).
- **"Afford anything, but not everything"** (Paula Pant). You can fund almost any single priority **if** you ruthlessly cut spending in the areas you *don't* value. Prioritize explicitly; don't try to have it all.
- **Gratitude and reference points.** Happiness is relative to who you compare yourself to. Curate that comparison set (and your media/social feeds) downward/sideways, practice gratitude, and you need less to feel content.
- **Relationships are the real superpower.** The strongest predictor of health and longevity isn't income — it's social connection. Treat investing in relationships as a financial-grade priority.
- **Rethink retirement.** Frame later life around **purpose, activity, learning, and connection**, not just a number; well-being and longevity track engagement, not idleness.

See `references/spending-for-wellbeing.md` for the specifics and the studies.

## Part 2 — Invest simply and cheaply, then leave it alone

The investing half (chapters 7–9, 11, appendix):

- **Fees are the silent killer.** Actively managed mutual funds charge an **expense ratio** (often plus advisor/trailing fees) that compounds against you; most active funds underperform a plain index after costs, and high turnover adds hidden drag. A percent or two a year is enormous over decades (ch. 7).
- **Buy the market: low-cost index funds / "couch potato" investing.** Own broad, diversified, low-fee **index funds/ETFs** across domestic + international stocks and bonds rather than picking stocks or funds. **All-in-one asset-allocation ETFs** (a single fund holding a fixed stock/bond mix, auto-rebalanced) and **robo-advisors** make this nearly effortless and cheap (ch. 8, appendix).
- **Behaviour beats brilliance — "set it and forget it."** The biggest returns come from automatic, regular contributions and **not** reacting to markets. Obsessing over the market correlates with worse outcomes *and* lower happiness; automate and disengage.
- **Pick an allocation you can actually hold.** Choose a stock/bond split that matches your **true risk tolerance and time horizon**, then rebalance mechanically. The right portfolio is the one you won't panic-sell in a crash — knowing yourself matters more than squeezing maximal return (ch. 9).
- **Socially responsible / ESG investing** is viable: low-cost SRI/ESG index funds let you align investments with values without necessarily sacrificing the passive, diversified approach (ch. 10).
- **Raise money-capable kids.** Chores (as "being a good human," not paid), modeling, delayed gratification, and involving them in real spending decisions build lifelong competence; use education-savings vehicles (RESP in Canada) early to harness compounding (ch. 11).

## Part 3 — The practical money toolkit (Alini)

Erica Alini's contribution is the hands-on, modern-economy mechanics — the script of "get a job, buy a house, retire at 65" no longer fits unstable work, sky-high housing, and student debt:

- **Debt is a tool, not evil — but a "frenemy."** Distinguish low-rate productive debt (mortgage, sometimes student loans) from corrosive high-rate debt (credit cards, payday loans); kill the high-interest debt first (avalanche vs. snowball — pick what you'll stick with). Understand interest/APR, minimum-payment traps, and how your **credit score** is built.
- **Housing is the biggest lever.** Shelter is usually your largest cost and gates everything else. **Rent vs. buy** isn't "renting is wasted money" — buying carries land-transfer tax, closing costs, ~1%/yr maintenance, property tax, interest, and illiquidity; renting + investing the difference can win, especially in expensive markets. If buying, understand mortgage mechanics (amortization, fixed/variable, the stress test, CMHC insurance under 20% down) and avoid being house-poor.
- **Income is half the equation.** Grow primary income (skills, negotiation, switching); judge **side hustles honestly** — net of tax, expenses, time, and burnout.
- **Prep for curveballs.** Build an **emergency fund**; carry the right **insurance** (renters insurance is cheap and skipped; **life and disability** matter once people depend on your income — your earning power is your biggest asset); get a **will and power of attorney** (yes, even if single).
- **Couples & family money.** Combining/separating finances, talking about money, the real **cost of kids** (childcare, parental leave/EI), saving early via **RESP**, and aging-parent/estate basics.

See `references/debt-housing-protection.md` for the full toolkit.

### When debt becomes a crisis: getting help (Canada)

If someone is struggling to keep up with payments, there's a structured ladder of help — and **talking to a non-profit credit counsellor is free and doesn't affect their credit score**:

- **Reach out early** (more options) and **prefer not-for-profit, accredited** agencies; the first consultation should be free.
- **Debt Management Plan (DMP)** — an *informal* plan via a credit counsellor: one monthly payment, often with interest reduced/stopped, repaying ~100% of principal over 3–5 years; covers **unsecured** debt only. Good fit if income is steady and interest relief makes repayment manageable; **not** a fit if the debt is too large to realistically repay, mostly secured, income is unstable, or someone wants a quick fix.
- **Consumer proposal / bankruptcy** — *formal, legal* processes through a **Licensed Insolvency Trustee** (free initial assessment) when a DMP isn't enough.
- **Avoid scams:** be wary of anyone promising to erase debt for "a fraction," "fix" a credit score fast, or claiming to be a "government program." Watch for-profit debt-settlement companies especially.

Be empathetic and non-judgmental — debt is tied to stress and shame. See `references/canadian-debt-help.md` for the FCAC guidance, how DMPs/proposals work, vetting an agency, and the resource list (Credit Counselling Canada, FCAC, OSB).

## Canadian context (verify current rules)

The book mentions Canadian wrappers: **RRSP** (tax-deferred retirement), **TFSA** (tax-free growth/withdrawals), **FHSA** (first-home savings), and **RESP** (education savings, with government grant matching). It also points to all-in-one ETFs and robo-advisors available to Canadians. **Do not state current contribution limits, grant rates, or tax treatment from memory** — these change yearly and vary by situation; look them up (or have the person confirm with CRA/an advisor). For the **authoritative CRA account mechanics** (FHSA $8k/$40k limits, RRSP deduction room, the Home Buyers' Plan $60k withdrawal and repayment), see [[canadian-registered-accounts]]. The *strategy* (max low-cost index exposure inside tax-advantaged accounts, automate, hold) is the durable takeaway.

## How to apply this skill

- Lead with the person's **values and goals**, then the money — Balance's whole point is that the spending/saving plan serves the life, not vice versa.
- For spending questions: which of the happiness-spending levers (experiences / others / time / small-frequent / anticipation) applies, and what to cut to "afford anything."
- For investing questions: steer toward **low fees, broad diversification, automation, and an allocation they can hold**; surface the factors rather than prescribing a specific product.
- Always keep the **not-advice** framing and flag anything region/time-specific as "verify current."
