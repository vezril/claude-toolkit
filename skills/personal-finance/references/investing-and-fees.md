# Investing, fees & kids (Balance, Part 2)

The investing chapters of *Balance*. Core message: **minimize costs, diversify broadly through index funds, automate, and hold an allocation you can stick with.** Behaviour, not brilliance, drives results. (Educational summary — not financial advice; verify current limits/rules/products for your region and year.)

## 7. Hidden fees drown out returns

- **How active funds cost you.** An actively managed mutual fund charges an annual **expense ratio** (a percentage of your assets) to pay the firm's costs and managers — deducted whether the fund wins or loses. On top, many investors pay **advisor/trailing commissions**. High **turnover** (managers trading most holdings within a year) adds transaction costs and tax drag.
- **Why it rarely pays.** The large majority of active funds **underperform a comparable low-cost index** over long periods, especially after fees; past outperformance doesn't predict future. The fund company's first priority is the firm, not the investor.
- **Fees compound against you.** A 1–2% annual fee sounds small but, compounded over 20–40 years, can consume a third or more of your potential ending wealth. Lowering cost is the most reliable "return" you control.

## 8. Set it and forget it — hands-off index investing

- **Buy the whole market.** Use broad, low-cost **index funds / ETFs** that track entire markets (domestic stocks, international stocks, bonds) instead of selecting individual stocks or active funds. Diversification + low fees + time is the engine.
- **The "couch potato" portfolio.** A simple fixed mix of a few index funds (or one all-in-one fund), rebalanced periodically. Boring and effective.
- **All-in-one asset-allocation ETFs.** A single ETF holding a set stock/bond ratio (e.g. conservative → aggressive variants) that **auto-rebalances** internally — one-fund, one-decision investing. Ideal for hands-off investors.
- **Robo-advisors.** Low-cost services that build and rebalance a diversified index portfolio for you at a fraction of traditional advisor fees — a middle ground between DIY ETFs and a full advisor.
- **Automate and disengage.** Set up automatic regular contributions and stop watching the market. Studies show preoccupation with money/markets reduces both returns (via reactive trading) and happiness. The discipline of *doing nothing* during volatility is the hard, valuable part.

## 9. Know yourself — choosing an allocation you'll hold

- The "best" allocation is the one you **won't abandon in a crash**. Match your stock/bond split to your **true risk tolerance** (how you actually behave when markets fall, not how brave you feel in calm times) and your **time horizon**.
- More stocks → higher expected return and bigger drawdowns; more bonds → smoother ride, lower expected return. Pick the point where you can stay invested through a 30–50% equity decline without selling.
- **Rebalance mechanically** (back to target weights on a schedule or threshold); this enforces "buy low, sell high" without judgment calls.

## 10. Socially responsible / ESG investing

Low-cost **SRI/ESG index funds and ETFs** let you align a portfolio with environmental/social values while keeping the passive, diversified, low-fee discipline. Screen for genuinely low costs and broad diversification; "values" shouldn't become an excuse for high fees or concentration.

## 11. Raising financially capable kids

- **Chores as "being a good human," not paid tasks** — builds responsibility and teamwork; doing chores in childhood correlates with later success.
- Model good behaviour, teach **delayed gratification**, and involve kids in **real spending decisions** (grocery prices, trade-offs).
- Start **education savings early** to harness compounding — in Canada the **RESP** attracts government grant matching (verify current grant/limit rules). The general principle: tax-advantaged, low-cost, automatic, started young.

## Canadian vehicles (look up current specifics)

The book references Canadian wrappers; use them to shelter low-cost index holdings:
- **RRSP** — contributions are tax-deductible, growth tax-deferred, withdrawals taxed (retirement).
- **TFSA** — contributions after-tax, growth and withdrawals tax-free (flexible).
- **RESP** — education savings with government grant top-ups.

**Do not quote current contribution limits, grant percentages, or tax rules from memory** — they change yearly and depend on the person's situation. Confirm with CRA or a qualified advisor. What's durable: *max out low-cost, broadly diversified index exposure inside the most tax-advantaged account available, automate it, and hold.*

## The one-paragraph version
Costs are the enemy and behaviour is the lever: own the whole market through cheap index funds (or one all-in-one ETF / a robo-advisor), inside tax-advantaged accounts, in a stock/bond mix you can hold through a crash; automate contributions, rebalance mechanically, ignore the noise, and start the kids early.
