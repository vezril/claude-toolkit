# Canadian investing platforms & accounts (RBC & BMO)

How to actually *hold* investments in Canada through the big banks — the account types, the service tiers, and the products — read directly off RBC and BMO's investment pages. The key lens (from the book sources): **the account is just a wrapper; what drives your long-run result is the cost of what's inside it and your behaviour.** So compare fees (MERs, commissions, management fees) across the tiers and lean toward low-cost, broad index exposure. (Educational, not advice; product names/fees as of 2026-06-03 — verify current details and fees with the bank.)

## The three (really four) service tiers — pick on cost & involvement

Both banks offer the same ladder; it's the single most important choice because it largely determines your fees:

1. **Self-directed (DIY online brokerage)** — *cheapest if you use low-cost ETFs.* You buy/sell your own stocks, ETFs, bonds, GICs.
   - **RBC Direct Investing** · **BMO InvestorLine Self-Directed**
   - Best paired with a couple of **low-cost index ETFs** or a single **all-in-one asset-allocation ETF** (auto-rebalancing). This is the lowest-fee path.
2. **Robo-advisor (managed, low-fee ETF portfolios)** — *low-effort middle ground.* You answer a risk questionnaire; they build and auto-rebalance a diversified **low-fee ETF** portfolio for a modest management fee.
   - **RBC InvestEase** · **BMO SmartFolio**
   - Cheaper than mutual funds/advisors, more hands-off than DIY — a good default for someone who won't manage it themselves.
3. **Online trading + advice** — e.g. **BMO InvestorLine adviceDirect** (self-directed trading with professional guidance/monitoring). A hybrid; check the fee.
4. **Advice / full-service** — in-branch investment professionals (often selling **mutual funds**) and, at higher asset levels, a **dedicated wealth advisor**. Most personal service, **highest cost** — watch the mutual-fund MERs and advisory fees here especially.

**Fee caution (the books' core warning):** bank-branch **mutual funds** often carry expense ratios well above index ETFs, and over decades that gap can consume a large share of returns. If someone is in high-MER funds, the highest-value move is usually shifting to low-cost index ETFs (DIY) or a robo-advisor — see `investing-and-fees.md`.

## Account types (held inside any tier)

You choose a **wrapper** (account) and then hold investments (ETFs, mutual funds, GICs, stocks, bonds) inside it. Both banks offer:

**Registered (tax-advantaged):**
- **TFSA** — tax-free growth & withdrawals.
- **RRSP** — tax-deductible contributions, tax-deferred growth.
- **FHSA** — first-home savings (deductible in *and* tax-free out).
- **RESP** — children's education, with government grant matching.
- **RRIF** — RRSP's withdrawal-phase successor (retirement income).
- **RDSP** — registered disability savings (BMO lists it explicitly; available broadly).

For the **rules, limits, and mechanics** of these (FHSA $8k/$40k, RRSP deduction room, the Home Buyers' Plan), see [[canadian-registered-accounts]] — and verify current figures with CRA.

**Non-registered:**
- **Cash account** — no tax shelter; capital gains, dividends, and interest are taxed yearly; contribute/withdraw freely.
- **Margin account** — a cash account that lets you borrow to invest (leverage = amplified risk; generally not advisable for ordinary savers).
- Plus locked-in (e.g. LIRA) and corporate/non-personal accounts for specific situations.

## Products you can hold

- **ETFs** — low-cost, diversified, exchange-traded; the workhorse for index investing (BMO is itself a major ETF issuer; RBC also offers ETFs). Prefer broad, low-MER index ETFs or all-in-one ETFs.
- **Mutual funds** — diversified and managed, but **watch the MER**; bank funds are frequently higher-cost than equivalent index ETFs.
- **GICs** — principal-guaranteed, fixed return; useful for short-term/known-date money and the safe sleeve, not long-term growth.
- **Stocks & bonds** — individual securities (DIY tiers); individual stock-picking is higher-risk and discouraged by the passive approach.
- **High-interest savings** — for the emergency fund / very short horizon, not investing.

## Matching account to goal (BMO's own framing, generalized)

- **Retirement** → RRSP, TFSA.
- **First-home down payment** → FHSA, TFSA, RRSP (Home Buyers' Plan), cash — and you can **stack FHSA + HBP** for one home (see [[canadian-registered-accounts]]).
- **Child's education** → RESP (grab the grant), TFSA.

## Putting it together (factors, not advice)

A common low-cost setup for a self-directed Canadian investor: open the **registered account** that fits the goal, inside a **self-directed brokerage** (RBC Direct Investing / BMO InvestorLine), and hold **one all-in-one index ETF** (or a few broad index ETFs) matched to a stock/bond allocation you can hold through a crash — then automate contributions and leave it alone. If self-managing isn't realistic, a **robo-advisor** (RBC InvestEase / BMO SmartFolio) delivers the same low-cost-index idea hands-off for a small fee. Reserve full-service advice for genuine complexity, and always **compare the all-in fees** first. Defer personalized decisions to a fee-only advisor.
