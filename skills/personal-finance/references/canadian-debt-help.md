# Getting help with debt in Canada

Authoritative resources and how the debt-relief options work, from the **Financial Consumer Agency of Canada (FCAC)** plus Canada's non-profit credit-counselling sector. For someone struggling with debt — especially if it's causing stress — this is the "where to turn and what to expect" reference. (Educational; not financial/legal advice. FCAC pages last modified Oct 2025; verify on canada.ca. Be especially gentle and non-judgmental here — debt is emotional.)

## First things to know

- **Talking to a credit counsellor does not affect your credit score.** A reputable agency's **first consultation is free** — no legitimate one charges for the first meeting.
- **Reach out early.** The sooner you ask for help, the more options you have. Financial difficulty is common (rising costs, high rates, life events) and nothing to be ashamed of.
- A credit counselling agency can offer **one-on-one counselling**, **budgeting courses/seminars**, and **debt management plans**.

## The spectrum of options (informal → formal)

1. **Do-it-yourself repayment** — budget, negotiate directly with creditors, debt consolidation loan. (FCAC: *Understanding debt*, *Paying back your debt*, *Limiting future debt*, *Debt consolidation*.)
2. **Debt Management Plan (DMP)** — *informal*, arranged through a (ideally non-profit) credit counsellor.
3. **Consumer proposal** — *formal, legal*, filed through a **Licensed Insolvency Trustee (LIT)**.
4. **Bankruptcy** — *formal, legal*, also through an LIT; last resort.

Everyone's situation differs — FCAC's advice is to **talk to more than one reputable source and compare** before deciding.

## Debt Management Plan (DMP) — how it works

An **informal proposal** your credit counsellor makes to your creditors on your behalf:
- Consolidates your debts into **one affordable monthly payment** to the agency, which distributes it to creditors.
- Creditors **often reduce or stop interest**; you'll **usually repay 100% of the principal**.
- **Covers unsecured debt** — credit cards, personal lines of credit, unsecured personal loans, collections. **Does not** usually cover **secured** debt (mortgage, car loan) — keep paying those.
- **Your responsibilities:** disclose all debts, pay on time, take on **no new credit**. Missing payments can cancel the plan.
- **Not legally binding on creditors:** some may decline (you'd then deal with them directly), and creditors/collection agencies aren't *legally forced* to stop collection — the counsellor can ask, but can't compel.
- **Fees vary** (setup, monthly admin, etc.). Compare the fees against the interest you'd save; if fees exceed savings, look elsewhere. Ask if fees can be reduced if you can't afford them.
- Usually runs **~3–5 years**. Ask for **regular written statements/receipts** confirming creditors are being paid on time.

### When a DMP makes sense (Credit Counselling Society / solveyourdebts.com)
- You **can afford to repay** the principal, and **interest relief** would make it manageable (e.g. payments currently swallowed by interest).
- You're **overwhelmed juggling multiple payments** and want it simplified to one.
- You have a **steady, reliable income** to sustain payments over 3–5 years.
- You want **support and accountability** (budget mentoring, someone in your corner).

### When a DMP is *not* the right fit
- Your **debt is too large** to realistically repay even with interest relief → a **consumer proposal or bankruptcy** may be more appropriate.
- Most of your debt is **secured** (mortgage/car) — a DMP won't cover it.
- Your **income is unstable** — a fixed monthly commitment may not work.
- You want a **quick fix** — a DMP takes commitment, consistency, and time; it's not a magic wand.

## Consumer proposal & bankruptcy (formal, legal)

For serious financial problems beyond a DMP, work with a **Licensed Insolvency Trustee (LIT)** — licensed by the **Office of the Superintendent of Bankruptcy (OSB)** to administer **consumer proposals** and **bankruptcies**, both legal processes. LITs typically provide a **free initial financial assessment**. A consumer proposal is a formal offer to creditors to settle for less than the full amount and/or on new terms; bankruptcy is the last-resort process for those who truly cannot repay. Compare via the **OSB "Compare debt solutions"** tool; find an LIT on the OSB registry.

Questions FCAC suggests asking about any option: how much of the debt is repaid, which debts, how long, monthly payment, what if you miss a payment or your situation changes, can creditors still contact you, what happens to your assets and your credit report, total fees, and whether a creditor can withdraw.

## Vetting an agency & avoiding scams

- **Prefer not-for-profit, accredited agencies.** Verify membership/good standing with a provincial or national association. Check for unresolved complaints via the **Better Business Bureau** and your **provincial/territorial consumer affairs office**.
- **Ask:** is the first consult free? what services? a written proposal? monthly statements? counsellor's qualifications/training/experience? Are you comfortable with them (you can request a different counsellor)?
- **Red flags / "too good to be true":** promises to erase debt for "a fraction" of what you owe, to "fix your credit score" quickly, or claims of being "part of a government program" (confirm directly with the government body). You can't legally erase accurate credit history; improving a score takes time and proven on-time habits. Reputable agencies never **coerce** you. Watch for-profit **debt settlement companies** especially carefully (FCAC: *Using a debt settlement company*).
- **Debt settlement ≠ non-profit credit counselling** — different model, often higher cost and risk.

## The human side

Debt is tied to stress, shame, and mental health (Credit Counselling Canada runs explicit "financial stress & mental health" resources). Treat people with empathy, never judgment; the practical message is that **free, confidential, non-profit help exists**, talking to them is free and won't hurt your credit, and reaching out early widens your options.
