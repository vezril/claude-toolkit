# The practical money toolkit (Money Like You Mean It)

From Erica Alini's *Money Like You Mean It* (Dundurn, 2021) — a **Canadian**, modern-economy personal-finance handbook. Its framing: the old script (get a job, buy a house, save, retire at 65) no longer fits a world of unstable work, sky-high housing, and student debt, so you need a new toolkit. Practical complement to Hallam's well-being/investing focus. (Educational summary — not advice; Canadian rules/figures change, **verify current specifics**.)

## 1. Mind over money (ch. 1)

Money behaviour is psychological. Avoidance (not opening bills, signing loan papers unread) and money anxiety sabotage decisions. The fix is awareness and systems, not shame — face the numbers, automate good defaults, and design around your own tendencies.

## 2. Debt is not created equal (ch. 2)

- **Debt is a tool, not evil — but a "frenemy."** You take it on to do something useful (own a home, get educated) with the explicit goal of getting rid of it. Interest is the price of using future money now.
- **Not all debt is equal.** Distinguish low-rate, potentially-productive debt (a mortgage, sometimes student loans) from high-rate, corrosive debt (credit-card balances, payday loans). Prioritize killing the high-interest debt first.
- **Understand the mechanics**: interest rate vs. APR, how credit-card interest compounds, minimum-payment traps, secured vs. unsecured, and how your **credit score** is built and why it matters (rates, approvals).
- **Payoff strategies**: avalanche (highest rate first — mathematically optimal) vs. snowball (smallest balance first — motivational). Pick the one you'll stick with; consider consolidation/refinancing where the rate genuinely drops.

## 3. Everything starts with housing (ch. 3)

- **Shelter is usually your largest cost**, and how much of your income it eats determines whether you can fund an emergency fund, retirement, or anything else. Controlling housing cost is the central financial challenge.
- **Rent vs. buy is not "renting is throwing money away."** Buying carries large, often-ignored costs (land transfer tax, closing costs, maintenance ~1%/yr, property tax, insurance, mortgage interest, illiquidity, opportunity cost of the down payment). Renting buys flexibility and frees capital to invest. Run the real numbers for *your* city and horizon; in expensive markets (Toronto/Vancouver and increasingly beyond) renting + investing the difference can win.
- **If buying**: understand mortgage mechanics (amortization, fixed vs. variable, term vs. amortization, the stress test, CMHC insurance on <20% down), don't become "house poor," and budget for the all-in cost, not just the mortgage payment.

## 4. Making money like you mean it (ch. 4)

Income is half the equation and the modern job market is unstable. Treat earning actively: grow your primary income (skills, negotiation, switching), and evaluate **side hustles / gig work honestly** — net of taxes, expenses, time, and burnout, is it actually worth it? Some side gigs pay poorly per hour once you account for everything; others build skills or become real income. Don't romanticize the hustle.

## 5. The big fat retirement myth (ch. 5)

The fixed "retire at 65 on a set number" model is increasingly unrealistic. Rethink retirement as **flexible and phased** — partial work, multiple income sources, and a target that reflects your real spending and longevity rather than a one-size number. (Dovetails with Hallam's purpose-driven retirement.)

## 6. Investing is not optional (ch. 6)

You cannot save your way to security at low interest rates — you must invest. Alini's practical take aligns with the index approach: low-cost, diversified, automated, inside tax-advantaged accounts. Use Canadian wrappers (**RRSP / TFSA**; **RESP** for kids) and low-cost vehicles (index ETFs, all-in-one ETFs, robo-advisors). (See `investing-and-fees.md`.)

## 7. Prepping for life's curveballs (ch. 7)

Protection is part of a financial plan:

- **Emergency fund** — cash buffer (commonly a few months' expenses) to absorb job loss or shocks without going into high-interest debt.
- **Insurance** — auto (mandatory in Canada), home/**renters** (renters insurance is cheap and widely skipped — covers belongings + liability), and crucially **life and disability** insurance if people depend on your income. Your ability to earn is often your biggest asset; insure it. Buy enough, and prefer simple term life over bundled products in most cases.
- **Will and power of attorney** — a will directs your assets; a **power of attorney** lets someone act for you if you're incapacitated. Important even for single people, not just parents.

## 8. Sharing money and paying for babies (ch. 8)

Money with a partner: how to combine or separate finances, talk about money, and handle imbalances. The real cost of children — childcare (a major Canadian expense), parental leave (EI considerations), and saving early via **RESP** to capture government grants. Verify current leave/benefit/grant rules.

## 9. All in the family (ch. 9)

Wider family money: helping or being helped by parents, aging-parent care, intergenerational support, and estate basics. Set expectations and document arrangements.

## The throughline
Alini pairs **macro context** (why housing, debt, and job precarity feel different now) with **concrete tactics**. The durable Canadian-toolkit takeaways: control housing cost, kill high-interest debt, build an emergency fund, insure your income and dependents, get a will + POA, invest cheaply inside RRSP/TFSA/RESP, and treat income growth as active work — all while remembering (per *Balance*) that the point is a good life, not a big number.
