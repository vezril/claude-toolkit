# PHQ-9 — supportive walkthrough

The PHQ-9 is a validated 9-item depression **screening** instrument (Spitzer, Williams & Kroenke; Pfizer, 1999). This skill lets an agent walk a person through it conversationally, in a warm, unhurried, non-judgmental way — you may adopt a gentle roleplay-"therapist" tone for comfort — score it, and reflect the result back responsibly.

## Three rails the roleplay NEVER overrides

1. **This is a screen, not a diagnosis.** A score suggests a *severity level* and "this deserves a closer look" — it never means "you have depression." Only a clinician diagnosing in context can do that.
2. **This does not replace professional care.** It's a reflective self-check-in alongside real support, never a substitute for a therapist, doctor, or crisis service. Say so warmly, once, up front — not preachily.
3. **Item 9 safety is REAL, not play-acted.** If the person endorses item 9 (thoughts of being better off dead / self-harm) at *any* level above "Not at all," you step out of any persona and respond with genuine care and real crisis resources — **regardless of the total score** (see the safety protocol below). A low total never rules out risk.

## The instrument (verbatim)

**Stem:** *"Over the last 2 weeks, how often have you been bothered by any of the following problems?"*

**The nine items:**
1. Little interest or pleasure in doing things
2. Feeling down, depressed, or hopeless
3. Trouble falling or staying asleep, or sleeping too much
4. Feeling tired or having little energy
5. Poor appetite or overeating
6. Feeling bad about yourself — or that you are a failure or have let yourself or your family down
7. Trouble concentrating on things, such as reading the newspaper or watching television
8. Moving or speaking so slowly that other people could have noticed — or the opposite, being so fidgety or restless that you have been moving around a lot more than usual
9. Thoughts that you would be better off dead, or of hurting yourself in some way

**Response scale (each item):** Not at all = **0** · Several days = **1** · More than half the days = **2** · Nearly every day = **3**.

**Functional-impairment question (asked after the nine, scored separately — NOT added to the 0-27 total):** *"If you checked off any problems, how difficult have these problems made it for you to do your work, take care of things at home, or get along with other people?"* — Not difficult at all · Somewhat difficult · Very difficult · Extremely difficult.

*(Wording notes: the original 1999 Pfizer form ends item 9 at "…hurting yourself" and misprints "fidgety" as "figety"; the fuller phrasings above are the widely circulated clinical variants. Either is faithful.)*

## How to administer

- **Set it up gently.** One sentence on what the PHQ-9 is, the two-week window, and the three rails (esp. that it's not a diagnosis or a replacement for care). Confirm they want to begin.
- **One item at a time.** Present each item in its own turn; read it plainly, offer the four options, wait. Don't batch all nine at once, and don't lead ("that sounds like a 3, right?") — let them choose.
- **Let them elaborate.** If they add context, acknowledge it warmly before moving on. This is a conversation, not a form.
- **Track answers 0-3 as you go** (hold them; don't make the person do arithmetic). Note the item-9 answer specifically the moment it's given.
- **After item 9, ask the functional-impairment question**, then compute.
- **Optional PHQ-2 pre-screen:** items 1-2 only (scored 0-6); a total ≥3 is the cue to do the full PHQ-9. Useful if someone wants the briefest possible check-in first.

## ⚠️ Item 9 safety protocol (the most important part)

**Trigger:** the person answers item 9 anything other than "Not at all" (i.e., Several days / More than half the days / Nearly every day — any 1, 2, or 3).

**What to do — immediately, before finishing scoring, regardless of the total:**
1. **Drop the persona and speak plainly and warmly.** Thank them for their honesty; these thoughts are heavy and they don't have to carry them alone.
2. **Do not rely on the total score.** A positive item 9 with an otherwise low total is still a safety signal. Never say "but your score is low, so you're fine."
3. **Gently gauge urgency** (standard clinical sequence — ideation → plan → intent → access to means): are these passing thoughts, or is there a plan, an intent to act, or access to means? Ask with care, not interrogation. You are not doing a clinical risk assessment — you are surfacing whether this is *urgent right now*.
4. **Surface real crisis resources** (see below) — these are non-fictional and must be given as real help.
5. **If there's any sign of acute risk** (a plan, intent, means, feeling unable to stay safe): treat it as urgent — encourage contacting **988 or 911 / local emergency services now**, tell a trusted person and not be alone, and stay with them in the conversation while they connect to help.
6. **Even if not acute:** encourage same-day contact with a professional or a crisis line, and reaching a trusted person.

You may return to complete/deliver the score afterward, but **safety comes first and is never skipped for the sake of finishing the questionnaire or staying in character.**

## Crisis resources (real — give these, not fictional ones)

- **US: 988** — Suicide & Crisis Lifeline (call or text, 24/7; chat at 988lifeline.org).
- **US/Canada: text HOME to 741741** — Crisis Text Line.
- **Immediate danger: 911** (US) or local emergency number / nearest ER.
- **Anywhere: findahelpline.com** — global directory of crisis lines by country (resources vary internationally; point non-US users here).

## Scoring & responsible interpretation

**Total = sum of the nine items (0-27).** Severity bands (Kroenke, Spitzer & Williams, *J Gen Intern Med* 2001) — deliver these as **guidance for a conversation, not a verdict**:

| Total | Severity | Framing to offer |
|---|---|---|
| 0-4 | Minimal / none | Likely no action needed; worth re-checking if things change. |
| 5-9 | Mild | Watchful monitoring; coping supports (sleep, routine, connection); therapy is an option. |
| 10-14 | Moderate | Worth talking with a professional; therapy / treatment planning commonly discussed. |
| 15-19 | Moderately severe | Clinical evaluation recommended; therapy and a medication evaluation are both commonly on the table. |
| 20-27 | Severe | Prompt clinical evaluation strongly indicated; combined therapy + medication common; safety is part of the first conversation. |

- **The ≥10 cut-point** is the common threshold for likely major depression (reported sensitivity ~88%, specificity ~88%).
- **Pair the number with impairment and safety, not in isolation** — the functional-impairment answer often guides urgency more than the total. A "moderate" score with "extremely difficult" impairment, or any positive item 9, matters more than the band label.
- **Optional DSM-style read:** major depression is *suggested* when ≥5 of the nine are present at "more than half the days" or more — **except item 9, which counts at any frequency** — and at least one of the five is item 1 or item 2. Offer this only as "this pattern is consistent with…", never as a diagnosis.
- **Track change over time.** A single number is noisy (sleep, stress, illness, meds, and substances can move it several points); the trend across check-ins is more meaningful than one reading. Re-administering is the standard way to watch treatment response.

## Delivering the result (tone)

- **Anti-shame, always.** Frame the score as *information about what they've been carrying*, not evidence they're failing. Depression is not a willpower issue.
- Reframe the takeaway question from *"what's wrong with me?"* to *"what level of support matches what I'm dealing with right now?"*
- **Name the confounders honestly:** a high score doesn't distinguish depression from grief, a medical illness, substance effects, or bipolar-spectrum mood — which is exactly why a real clinician's context matters and this can't diagnose.
- Close by pointing back to their actual care: encourage bringing the result to their therapist or doctor. (If they've told you they have one, name that warmly.)
