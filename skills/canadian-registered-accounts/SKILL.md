# Canadian Registered Accounts (FHSA, RRSP, HBP)

The mechanics of Canada's main tax-advantaged personal accounts and first-home programs, drawn directly from the **Canada Revenue Agency (CRA)** pages. This is the authoritative, rules-and-numbers complement to the behaviour/strategy in [[personal-finance]] (which deliberately avoids quoting limits).

> **Two standing cautions.**
> 1. **Not tax advice.** Claude is not a tax advisor. This skill states the published rules so a person can understand their options; for personalized or complex situations (cross-border, estates, over-contributions, marriage breakdown) point them to the **CRA** or a qualified tax professional/accountant.
> 2. **Figures change — verify.** Dollar limits are periodically indexed and rules get amended (often at the federal budget). **Every figure below is stamped "as of 2026-06" from CRA pages last modified Jan–Feb 2026.** Before relying on a number, re-check the linked CRA page (or the person's CRA account / Notice of Assessment, which shows their actual personal room).

## FHSA — First Home Savings Account

A registered plan that lets a **first-time home buyer** save for a qualifying first home **tax-free**: contributions are **tax-deductible** (like an RRSP) *and* qualifying withdrawals are **tax-free** (like a TFSA) — the best of both.

- **Participation room:** **$8,000** in the first year you open an FHSA. (as of 2026-06)
- **Lifetime limit:** **$40,000** total. (as of 2026-06)
- **Carry-forward:** unused room carries forward, but only up to **$8,000** can be carried into a later year — so the most you can put in within a single year is **$16,000** (this year's $8,000 + one year's $8,000 carry-forward). Carry-forward only starts accruing **after** you open your first FHSA.
- **Combined cap:** your annual room applies to **contributions + transfers from your RRSPs combined**, across **all** your FHSAs. RRSP→FHSA transfers use room but are **not deductible** (you already got the deduction on the RRSP).
- **Investment growth** inside the FHSA does **not** use up room.
- **Over-contribution:** an excess FHSA amount is taxed **1% per month** on the highest excess in the month (Form RC728).
- **Reporting:** file **Schedule 15** with your return for the year you open your first FHSA (even if you contribute nothing); the issuer sends a **T4FHSA**.
- **Qualifying withdrawal:** tax-free if you meet first-time-buyer + qualifying-home conditions. **You can do an FHSA qualifying withdrawal AND an RRSP Home Buyers' Plan withdrawal for the same home.**
- **Maximum participation period:** the FHSA must be closed by year-end after the earliest of the 15th anniversary of opening, the year you turn 71, or the year after your first qualifying withdrawal — at which point unused funds generally transfer to an RRSP/RRIF (tax-deferred) or are withdrawn (taxable).

See `references/fhsa.md` for room calculation, examples, transfers, and closing rules.

## RRSP — Registered Retirement Savings Plan

A retirement plan you (or your spouse/common-law partner) contribute to. **Deductible contributions reduce taxable income**; investment income is **tax-deferred** while inside; **withdrawals are taxed** as income.

- **Deduction limit:** **18% of your previous year's earned income**, up to an **annual dollar maximum** that is indexed each year, **plus** unused room carried forward, **minus** any pension adjustment. (Don't quote the current-year max from memory — it changes annually; check the person's Notice of Assessment / CRA account.)
- **Spousal RRSP:** you contribute to your spouse's RRSP and claim the deduction yourself (income-splitting tool; attribution rules apply).
- **Age limit:** you **cannot contribute after the end of the year you turn 71**; the RRSP must be wound up then (typically converted to a **RRIF** or annuity).
- **Withdrawals** are taxable and the room is generally **not** restored — except under the two programs that allow temporary tax-free withdrawals: the **Home Buyers' Plan (HBP)** and the **Lifelong Learning Plan (LLP)**.

See `references/rrsp-and-hbp.md` for details.

## HBP — Home Buyers' Plan

Lets you **withdraw from your RRSP tax-free to buy or build a qualifying first home** (for yourself or a specified disabled person), then pay it back.

- **Withdrawal limit:** **$60,000** (as of 2026-06; raised from $35,000 for withdrawals after April 16, 2024). Per person, so a couple could withdraw up to $60,000 each.
- **Repayment:** repay over **up to 15 years**, a minimum of **1/15 of the amount per year**, back into an RRSP/PRPP/SPP. Miss a year's minimum and that portion is added to your taxable income (line 12900).
- **When repayment starts:** the **2nd year after** the year of your first withdrawal — **except** a temporary relief defers it to the **5th year after** for first withdrawals made **between Jan 1, 2022 and Dec 31, 2025**. (e.g. first withdrawal in 2023 → first repayment year 2028.) (as of 2026-06)
- **Contribution-then-withdraw rule:** RRSP contributions generally must stay in the plan **at least 90 days** before an HBP withdrawal to remain deductible (verify on CRA).
- **Combine with FHSA:** you may use **both** the HBP and an FHSA qualifying withdrawal for the **same** home.
- **Forms:** T1036 to withdraw; Schedule 7 to designate repayments.

See `references/rrsp-and-hbp.md` for repayment math and special situations (age 71, death, becoming a non-resident).

## Choosing between them for a first home (factors, not advice)

- **FHSA** is uniquely powerful for a first home: deductible going in *and* tax-free coming out, and you don't have to repay it. Limited to $8k/yr, $40k lifetime.
- **HBP** unlocks money already in an RRSP without immediate tax, but you **must repay it** over 15 years or it becomes taxable income — it's a loan from your future self.
- **TFSA** withdrawals are tax-free and flexible (no repayment, no first-home restriction) but contributions aren't deductible.
- Many first-time buyers **stack** FHSA + HBP for the same purchase. The right mix depends on income (deduction value), timeline, and existing balances — surface these trade-offs; don't prescribe.
