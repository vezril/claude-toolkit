# RRSP & the Home Buyers' Plan (CRA detail)

From the CRA RRSP and HBP pages (RRSP page modified 2025-01-02; HBP pages modified Jan–Feb 2026). Figures **as of 2026-06** — re-verify on canada.ca.

## RRSP — Registered Retirement Savings Plan

A plan you (or your spouse/common-law partner) contribute to and the CRA registers.

- **Tax treatment:** contributions are **deductible** (reduce taxable income); income earned inside is **tax-deferred** while it stays in the plan; **withdrawals are taxed** as ordinary income (and the room is generally not given back).
- **Deduction limit (contribution room):** **18% of your previous year's earned income**, up to an **annual dollar maximum** that is **indexed yearly**, **plus** your unused deduction room carried forward, **minus** your pension adjustment (if you have a workplace pension). Your exact room is on your **Notice of Assessment** / CRA account — use that, not a remembered number.
- **Spousal RRSP:** you contribute to your spouse's/CLP's RRSP and **you** take the deduction — an income-splitting strategy; attribution rules apply if funds are withdrawn too soon.
- **Age 71 wind-up:** you **cannot contribute after the end of the year you turn 71**; the RRSP must be converted (usually to a **RRIF** or an annuity) by then.
- **Tax-free withdrawal programs:** the only ways to pull money out without immediate tax are the **Home Buyers' Plan (HBP)** and the **Lifelong Learning Plan (LLP)** — both are loans you repay to the RRSP.
- Setting up, transferring, receiving income (RRIF/annuity), and death-of-annuitant rules each have their own CRA pages.

## HBP — Home Buyers' Plan

Withdraw from your RRSP, tax-free, to **buy or build a qualifying home** for yourself or a **specified disabled person**, then repay it.

### The numbers (as of 2026-06)
- **Withdrawal limit: $60,000** (raised from $35,000 for withdrawals after April 16, 2024). It's **per individual**, so two eligible buyers could each withdraw up to $60,000.
- **Repay over up to 15 years**, minimum **1/15 of the withdrawn amount per year**, by contributing to an RRSP/PRPP/SPP and designating it as a repayment (Schedule 7, line 24600).
- **Combine with FHSA:** you may withdraw under the HBP **and** make an FHSA qualifying withdrawal for the **same** qualifying home (meeting each program's conditions).

### When repayment starts
- First withdrawal **before Jan 1, 2022:** repayment starts the **2nd year after** the withdrawal year (e.g. 2020 → 2022).
- **Temporary relief:** for a first withdrawal **between Jan 1, 2022 and Dec 31, 2025**, the start is deferred by 3 extra years — repayment begins the **5th year after** the withdrawal year (e.g. 2023 → 2028).
- You may repay **early**; the period stays the same but early repayments reduce later minimums.

### If you repay less than the minimum
The shortfall (minimum required minus what you designated) is included as **RRSP income on line 12900** for that year. You still owe the remaining annual repayments until the HBP balance is zero.

### Eligibility & process
- You must be a **first-time home buyer** (similar four-year look-back as the FHSA), a **resident of Canada**, with a **written agreement** to buy/build, intending to occupy as principal residence within one year.
- RRSP contributions generally must be in the plan **≥ 90 days** before the HBP withdrawal to be deductible (verify on CRA).
- Use **Form T1036** to withdraw.

### Repayment math example (CRA)
Withdraw $30,000 in 2023 → minimum **$2,000/yr** ($30,000 ÷ 15) starting 2028 (temporary relief). If in a later year you repay more than the minimum (say $8,000), the remaining balance is divided by the **years remaining** to set the next minimum.

### Special situations
- **Age 71:** you can't contribute to an RRSP after the year you turn 71, so you can't make further HBP repayments — the remaining balance is brought into income over the remaining years (or repaid in full by then).
- **Death:** the remaining HBP balance is generally added to the deceased's income for the year of death, unless a surviving spouse/CLP **jointly elects** (Form RC98) to assume the repayments.
- **Becoming a non-resident:** repay the balance (by the earlier of filing your return for the year you become non-resident or 60 days after), or include it in income; special rules if you emigrate before buying.

### Important limits
- You **cannot** repay an HBP withdrawal into an **FHSA** — it must go to an RRSP/PRPP/SPP.
- Amounts designated as **repayments are not deductible** again.

## RRSP vs. FHSA vs. TFSA for a down payment (factors, not advice)
- **HBP (RRSP):** big lump available, tax-free up front, but **must be repaid** over 15 years or taxed — effectively borrowing from your retirement.
- **FHSA:** deductible in **and** tax-free out, **no repayment**, but capped ($8k/yr, $40k lifetime) and first-home-only.
- **TFSA:** tax-free and flexible, no repayment or first-home rule, but **not deductible**.
- Stacking **FHSA + HBP** for one home is common. Weigh income (deduction value), timeline, and balances — present the trade-offs; defer personalized decisions to CRA / a tax professional.
