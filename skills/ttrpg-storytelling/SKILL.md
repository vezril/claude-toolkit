# TTRPG storytelling

How to write and run stories for tabletop roleplaying games. The load-bearing idea, and the one every source below converges on: **a TTRPG story is co-authored at the table, not delivered to it.** You prepare a situation rich enough to survive contact with player choices, then improvise the telling. Prep is scaffolding; the game is the story.

## The stance (get this right and the rest follows)

- **Prep situations, not plots.** Design a compelling scenario with multiple valid solutions — a villain with a plan, a place under pressure, people who want conflicting things — not a linear sequence of events the players must walk through. A plot breaks the moment players do something unplanned; a situation just reacts. *(ravenousrpg)*
- **Players are co-authors.** "Plan key story beats but expect detours." Your prep provides structure; the players provide the surprises, and the surprises are the point. Treat railroading (forcing a predetermined outcome) as the failure mode to design against. *(rpgstorytelling, ttrpgs.co.uk)*
- **Know your table.** Learn what *this* group enjoys — political intrigue, tactical combat, exploration, character drama, comedy — and weight the story toward it. The best-written story aimed at the wrong table falls flat. *(ttrpgs.co.uk)*
- **Establish it in Session Zero.** Before writing the campaign, run a session zero: agree on tone, content lines (hard no's) and veils (offscreen it), the pitch, and the kind of characters that fit. Put safety tools on the table — **Lines & Veils** and an **X-card** (anyone can pause/skip a moment, no explanation owed). Collaborative storytelling only works when everyone feels safe contributing. *(established practice; the source guides flag its absence)*

## Writing the story (prep)

Work outside-in, then assemble. *(tangibleday's stages, sharpened.)*

1. **World / context.** Setting, era, cultures, the magic/tech logic, and the pressure the world is already under. Rich context is what gives player decisions *meaning* — it's the "C" of Context. Don't build the whole world; build the corner the story happens in, in sensory detail (below).
2. **Conflict.** Pick the engine. Every campaign runs on conflict, external (battles, mysteries, factions) **and** internal (a character's doubt, fear, guilt). The four classic axes — **person vs. person / nature / society / self** — are a fast way to check you have real stakes and, ideally, more than one axis in play. *(tangibleday, rpgstorytelling)*
3. **The people.** Build the NPCs and the villain with **quirks, motivations, and secrets** — the trio that makes an NPC memorable and investable. The villain especially needs a *plan* (what happens if the players do nothing?), a *goal* the players can understand, and a reason they're hard to simply kill. *(rpgstorytelling)*
4. **Assemble the arc.** Give it a beginning (introduce the conflict and the people), a middle (explore and complicate it — the bulk), and an end (resolve it with a sense of finality). Scale this fractally: the **campaign** is an arc of **arcs**, each arc an arc of **sessions**, each session its own beginning/middle/end. *(tangibleday)*

Run the whole thing through **the 5 C's** as a quality check — a good story has all five: **Character** (compelling goals, authentic flaws), **Conflict** (external + internal), **Context** (worldbuilding that gives decisions meaning), **Climax** (high-stakes payoff for the investment), **Change** (characters and world transform by the end). Miss one and you can usually name what feels flat. *(rpgstorytelling)*

**Hooks & agency.** Give players choices that actually bend the story — side with this faction or that one, and mean it. A hook works when it engages what a *character* wants, not just "a job is available." And leave the plot loose enough to change: "don't hesitate to change" a plotline the table has clearly voted against with their choices.

## Running it (at the table)

- **Open with a Bang.** Start scenes *in medias res* with immediate pressure, not "you arrive at the tavern, what do you do?" — "The tavern is already on fire when you arrive." "A strong opening isn't about description; it's about presenting a loaded question the table must answer." Keep a **Bang Bank**: a list of generic high-pressure starters (a mysterious note, trembling ground, a stranger with a wanted poster) to inject when play sags. *(ravenousrpg)*
- **Build the shared image (equifinality).** Everyone must picture the same space or immersion breaks. Lead with specific sensory detail — "the statue is cracked, its left arm missing; the air smells of wet stone and ozone" — and pull players into the description: "What detail catches your eye first?" Sound and smell do more than sight: distant thunder, the stillness of an ancient temple, a market's reek. *(ravenousrpg, ttrpgs.co.uk)*
- **Share the narration — strategically.** After a player wins a roll, hand them the telling: "Describe the finishing blow." "How does the crowd react to your speech?" It deepens investment and keeps them in-character. But keep world-building and the core mysteries yours — don't pass the conch for *every* scene; that dilutes your authorship where it matters. *(ravenousrpg)*
- **Loop choices into consequences.** Keep light notes on key NPCs/locations and ask "if the players help this one, how does it repay them? if they insult that one, how does it come back?" — then *revisit it later*. This is what makes a world feel alive and proves the story is theirs: "It validates player agency. It tells them the story is truly theirs." *(ravenousrpg)*
- **Pace with contrast.** Alternate intense action with quieter character beats; the quiet is where investment for the next spike gets built. Surprise, tension, and growth are the three things a session should deliver — twists that are *unexpected but believable within the world's logic*, and consequences characters actually grow through. *(rpgstorytelling, ttrpgs.co.uk)*
- **Constraints are fuel (the paradox of structure).** Your system's rules, the map, the character sheet — treat them as creative scaffolds, not walls. "Any system or framework is both enabling and limiting; the magic happens when you lean into that." A tight constraint forces the interesting choice. *(ravenousrpg)*
- **Improvise on the frame.** "A great storyteller can improvise and incorporate new ideas while maintaining the narrative's overall structure." When players go sideways, keep the *situation's* pressures and let the events reshape freely. *(ttrpgs.co.uk)*

## The connective tissue

- **Foreshadow, then pay off.** Plant subtle hints of what's coming; the payoff lands because the table half-saw it. *(rpgstorytelling)*
- **End on a cliffhanger.** Cut the session on a suspended question — they'll arrive next time already leaning in. *(rpgstorytelling)*
- **Engage directly.** Ask the reflective, in-character questions that turn spectators into authors: "What does your character think about this?" Silence at the table is usually a cue to ask one. *(rpgstorytelling)*
- **Props and sound.** Maps, handouts, and ambient music are cheap immersion multipliers — reach for them at the set-piece moments. *(rpgstorytelling)*

## Diagnosing a game that isn't working

- *Players passive / low engagement* → open scenes with a Bang; ask "what does your character think?"; check the hook engages a *want*, not just an errand.
- *Feels railroady* → you prepped a plot, not a situation; re-frame the next beat as a pressure with multiple solutions and let choices bend it.
- *World feels flat/generic* → thin Context or no sensory detail; add smell/sound and one specific broken detail per location.
- *Choices feel weightless* → you're not looping; start tracking consequences and revisit one within a session or two.
- *Villain is boring* → missing plan/goal/secret; give them something that happens if the party does nothing.

## Always-apply

1. Prep the situation, improvise the telling. Never write a plot the players must obey.
2. Run every campaign/arc/session through beginning-middle-end and the 5 C's.
3. Open with pressure, close on a cliffhanger, and loop their choices back at them.
4. Session zero + safety tools first — collaboration needs a safe table.
5. Serve *this* table's tastes over the "objectively good" story.
