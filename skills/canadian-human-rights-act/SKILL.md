# The Canadian Human Rights Act (H-6)

The **federal** anti-discrimination statute, enforced by the **Canadian Human Rights Commission (CHRC)** and the **Canadian Human Rights Tribunal (CHRT)**.

> ## ⚠️ Read the scope question first — most people get this wrong
> **The CHRA probably doesn't apply to your problem.** It reaches only what **Parliament** can legislate. If your landlord, employer, or service provider is **provincially regulated** — which is nearly all of them — your instrument is your **province's** human-rights law. **In Quebec that's the [[quebec-charter-rights]] Charter (C-12), via the CDPDJ.**

> ## Information, not legal advice
> I'm not a lawyer. This maps the statute so you can find the right forum and read the real text. Quoted from the official consolidation, retrieved **2026-07** — verify currency.

## The scope trap, precisely

**There is no list of covered industries in the Act.** None. The familiar "banks, airlines, railways, telecoms" enumeration is **correct but external** — it comes from **s. 91 of the *Constitution Act, 1867*** and case law, **not** from a CHRA section. *Anyone who cites you a section number for such a list is inventing it.*

The scope is set by **s. 2**:

> "The purpose of this Act is to extend the laws in Canada to give effect, **within the purview of matters coming within the legislative authority of Parliament**, to the principle that all individuals should have an opportunity equal with other individuals to make for themselves the lives that they are able and wish to have **and to have their needs accommodated**…"

That bolded clause is the whole jurisdictional boundary — a **constitutional limit**, not a policy choice. **s. 66(1)** confirms the Act binds the Crown "**in right of Canada**" only. And the Act repeatedly assumes a parallel provincial regime it doesn't touch: **s. 27(1)(c)** (liaise with provincial bodies "to **avoid conflicts respecting the handling of complaints in cases of overlapping jurisdiction**"), **s. 28(2)**, **s. 41(1)(c)**.

### The housing example — the chain, from the statute

1. **s. 6 does prohibit housing discrimination**: "It is a discriminatory practice in the provision of **commercial premises or residential accommodation** (a) to deny occupancy… or (b) to differentiate adversely… on a prohibited ground."
2. **But s. 2 caps s. 6** at matters within Parliament's authority. Residential tenancy is **property and civil rights in the province** — s. 92(13). A private landlord is not a federal undertaking.
3. So the Commission **must decline** under **s. 41(1)(c)** — beyond its jurisdiction.

**Result: s. 6 is close to a dead letter for ordinary housing.** It bites only where the landlord *is* federal — military housing, a federal Crown corporation's stock, or on-reserve housing administered by a First Nation government. **In Quebec, go to the CDPDJ under C-12**, whose ss. 12/13/15 reach private landlords directly.

**First Nations governments are in scope** since the 2008 repeal of s. 67, subject to **s. 1.2**, which directs interpretation "in a manner that gives due regard to **First Nations legal traditions and customary laws**, particularly the balancing of individual rights and interests against collective rights and interests, to the extent that they are consistent with the **principle of gender equality**."

## s. 3 — the prohibited grounds (verbatim)

> "…the prohibited grounds of discrimination are **race, national or ethnic origin, colour, religion, age, sex, sexual orientation, gender identity or expression, marital status, family status, genetic characteristics, disability and conviction for an offence for which a pardon has been granted or in respect of which a record suspension has been ordered**."

**s. 3(2)** — pregnancy or child-birth is **deemed** discrimination on the ground of **sex**. **s. 3(3)** — refusing a **genetic test** or to disclose its results is deemed **genetic characteristics**. **s. 3.1 — intersectionality:** a practice includes one based on "**the effect of a combination of prohibited grounds**."

*(Trail: sexual orientation 1996; gender identity or expression and genetic characteristics both 2017.)*

## The discriminatory practices (ss. 5–14.1)

| s. | Practice |
|---|---|
| **5** | Goods, services, facilities or accommodation **"customarily available to the general public"** |
| **6** | **Commercial premises or residential accommodation** *(but see the scope cap)* |
| **7** | **Employment** — "directly or indirectly", refusing to employ or adverse differentiation in the course of employment |
| **8** | Application forms, advertisements, inquiries expressing a "limitation, specification or preference" |
| **9** | Employee organizations |
| **10** | **Systemic** — a policy or practice that "**deprives or tends to deprive** an individual or class" of employment opportunities |
| **11** | **Equal wages** for "**work of equal value**" in the same establishment — criterion is "the composite of the **skill, effort and responsibility** required… and the conditions under which the work is performed"; **11(5)** "sex does not constitute a reasonable factor justifying a difference in wages"; **11(6)** no reducing wages to comply |
| **12** | Discriminatory notices/signs |
| **13** | **[Repealed, 2013]** — the former internet hate-speech provision |
| **14** | **Harassment** on a prohibited ground, in services, premises/accommodation, or employment — **14(2)**: **sexual harassment** is deemed harassment on a prohibited ground |
| **14.1** | **Retaliation** against a complainant — a **freestanding** practice needing no ground of its own |

⚠️ **s. 40.2** — the Commission has **no jurisdiction** over pay-equity complaints against employers subject to the ***Pay Equity Act***; s. 11 is displaced for them.

## s. 15 — exceptions and the duty to accommodate

**s. 15(1)** — not discriminatory where based on a **bona fide occupational requirement** (a), or with **bona fide justification** for a denial under ss. 5/6 (g), plus age-limit, pension, guideline, and pregnancy/childcare-benefit carve-outs. *(15(1)(c), the mandatory-retirement exception, was repealed in 2011.)*

**s. 15(2) — the key provision:**

> "For any practice mentioned in paragraph (1)(a) to be considered to be based on a bona fide occupational requirement and for any practice mentioned in paragraph (1)(g) to be considered to have a bona fide justification, **it must be established that accommodation of the needs of an individual or a class of individuals affected would impose undue hardship on the person who would have to accommodate those needs, considering health, safety and cost**."

**Three statutory factors only — health, safety, cost.** The burden is on the party claiming the exception. **Accommodation is built into the defence**: a BFOR/BFJ *fails* unless accommodation would cause undue hardship.

**s. 15(8)** — applies "**regardless of whether it results in direct discrimination or adverse effect discrimination**" (the unified test, codified). **s. 15(9)** — subject to the **universality of service** principle for the Canadian Forces: a statutory ceiling on accommodation there.

## Process — and the gate

**s. 40(1)** — anyone with "**reasonable grounds for believing**" may file. **s. 40(5)** is an absolute territorial/status bar. **s. 41(1)** — the Commission **shall** deal with a complaint **unless** (a) other procedures weren't exhausted; (b) better dealt with under another federal Act; **(c) beyond its jurisdiction**; (d) "trivial, frivolous, vexatious or made in bad faith"; or **(e)** the limitation below.

**s. 41(1)(e) — the limitation:**
> "…the complaint is based on acts or omissions **the last of which occurred more than one year**, or **such longer period of time as the Commission considers appropriate in the circumstances**, before receipt of the complaint."

**One year, running from the *last* act** (so a continuing course of conduct restarts it) — and it's **discretionary, not a hard bar**. Contrast **s. 40(5)**, which *is* absolute.

**s. 49(1) — the gate:** "**At any stage after the filing** of a complaint, **the Commission may request** the Chairperson of the Tribunal to institute an inquiry…" **Only the Commission can refer.** There is **no direct right of access** to the CHRT — unlike Quebec's C-12, which at least gives a declined complainant the s. 84 ninety-day substitution.

**s. 52(1)** — "An inquiry shall be conducted **in public**."

## s. 53 — remedies (and the caps)

| Head | Amount |
|---|---|
| **Cease** the practice + measures to redress/prevent (incl. special programs) — **53(2)(a)** | *uncapped* |
| Make available the denied rights/opportunities — **(b)** | — |
| **Lost wages** + expenses — **(c)** | **uncapped** |
| Additional costs of alternative goods/services/accommodation — **(d)** | uncapped |
| **Pain and suffering** — **(e)** | **max $20,000** |
| **Special compensation** for **wilful or reckless** discrimination — **53(3)** | **max $20,000** |
| Interest — **53(4)** | — |

**Non-pecuniary ceiling: $40,000.** Both figures date from **1998** and are **not indexed** — nothing in the Act adjusts them. What's *uncapped* is lost wages and systemic orders. **s. 54** — no order may remove someone who took a job **in good faith**, or expel an occupant who obtained accommodation in good faith. **s. 57** — orders become enforceable as **Federal Court** orders.

## Fast answers

| Question | Answer |
|---|---|
| Does it cover my landlord? | **Almost certainly not.** Unless the landlord is federal. In Quebec → **CDPDJ under C-12**. |
| Does it cover my job? | Only if your employer is **federally regulated** (bank, airline, railway, telecom, federal department/Crown corp, First Nation government). |
| Which section lists the covered industries? | **None** — that's the trap. Scope is **s. 2** + the Constitution. |
| How long do I have? | **One year from the last act** (s. 41(1)(e)) — extendable at the Commission's discretion. |
| Can I go straight to the Tribunal? | **No.** **s. 49** — only the Commission refers. |
| Max payout for hurt feelings? | **$20,000** (s. 53(2)(e)) + **$20,000** if wilful/reckless (s. 53(3)) = **$40,000**, unindexed since 1998. Lost wages are separate and uncapped. |
| Is retaliation covered? | **Yes** — **s. 14.1**, freestanding. |
