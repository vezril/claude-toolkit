# Charter of human rights and freedoms (Quebec, C-12)

Quebec's own rights charter — enacted **1975**, in force **28 June 1976**, nearly a decade before the Canadian Charter. It is **quasi-constitutional**: it binds the State (s. 54), reaches **private conduct**, and **overrides inconsistent Quebec legislation** within its primacy range. For most everyday rights questions in Quebec — a discriminating landlord, employer, or business — **this, not the Canadian Charter, is the instrument that applies.**

## The two facts that change everything

**1. It applies horizontally — to private actors.** **s. 55**: "The Charter affects those matters that come under the **legislative authority of Québec**." Combined with the "**No one may…**" drafting of ss. 10–19, that reaches **private employers, landlords, and businesses**. The Canadian Charter binds only government (s. 32). This is why a Quebec tenant facing a discriminating private landlord has a real Charter claim — under **C-12**, not the federal one, and not the [[canadian-human-rights-act]] (which is capped at federal jurisdiction).

**2. Primacy covers ss. 1–38 only.** **s. 52**: no Act may derogate from **ss. 1 to 38** "unless such Act **expressly states that it applies despite the Charter**." So:

| Chapter | Sections | Primacy over legislation? |
|---|---|---|
| I — Fundamental freedoms | 1–9.1 | ✅ |
| I.1 — Equality | 10–20.1 | ✅ |
| II — Political rights | 21–22 | ✅ |
| III — Judicial rights | 23–38 | ✅ |
| **IV — Economic and social rights** | **39–48** | ❌ **No** |
| V — Remedies/interpretation | 49–56 | ❌ |

The economic and social rights (free education, an acceptable standard of living, a healthful environment, protection of the aged from exploitation) are **declaratory against the legislature** — but still **actionable under s. 49 against private parties**.

## s. 10 — the grounds (closed list)

> race · colour · **sex** · **gender identity or expression** · **pregnancy** · sexual orientation · **civil status** · **age except as provided by law** · religion · **political convictions** · **language** · ethnic or national origin · **social condition** · **a handicap or the use of any means to palliate a handicap**

**Closed** — no analogous-grounds doctrine (unlike Canadian Charter s. 15). **Bolded** = no federal-Charter counterpart. *Social condition* (income, employment, education) is the one that most often matters in housing. **s. 10 is not freestanding** — it attaches to another right or to ss. 10.1–19.

## s. 49 — what you can actually get

| Remedy | Threshold |
|---|---|
| **Cessation** of the interference | **unlawful** interference |
| **Compensatory damages** (moral or material prejudice) | **unlawful** interference |
| **Punitive damages** | interference that is **unlawful *and* intentional** — and the tribunal **"may"** (discretionary) |

## Fast answers

| Question | Answer |
|---|---|
| Does it bind my private landlord/employer? | **Yes** — s. 55 + ss. 10–19. That's the big difference from the Canadian Charter. |
| Is "social condition" (being on assistance / low income) a ground? | **Yes** — a Quebec-only ground. |
| Can I go straight to the Human Rights Tribunal? | **No.** **s. 111** — only the Commission may initiate. If it declines, **s. 84** gives you **90 days, at your own expense**, to substitute yourself. |
| How long do I have to complain to the CDPDJ? | No hard bar, but **s. 77** lets it refuse where the last act was **more than 2 years** before filing. |
| Does complaining protect my civil lawsuit deadline? | **Yes** — **s. 76 suspends prescription** while the Commission has the file. |
| What does the Commission actually investigate? | Only **ss. 10–19 discrimination**, **s. 48(1) exploitation** of aged/handicapped persons, and **s. 82 reprisals**. Everything else you take to the ordinary courts under s. 49. |
| Is there a duty to help someone in danger? | **Yes** — **s. 2**, a genuine Quebec-only positive duty to rescue. |

## Gotchas

- **The Commission's mandate is narrower than the Charter.** ss. 1–9 and Ch. III/IV violations are **not** investigated by the CDPDJ — those are s. 49 claims in the ordinary courts. People file with the Commission and get bounced for this.
- **s. 84's 90 days is a hard gate.** If the Commission declines and you sit on it, the Tribunal closes to you.
- **Ch. IV rights don't beat legislation.** Quoting s. 45 ("acceptable standard of living") at a statute won't work — no s. 52 primacy.
- **Punitive damages need intent**, not just a violation.
- **s. 9.1 limits ss. 1–9 only** — not the s. 10 equality right (which is limited via ss. 20/20.1 instead).
- **"Age except as provided by law"** is a built-in limit unique to that ground.
